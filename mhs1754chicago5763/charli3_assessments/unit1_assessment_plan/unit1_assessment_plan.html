Conceptual Physics — Unit 1 Assessment Plan

Unit 1: Forces, Motion, and Equilibrium

Essential Standard

Students will analyze forces, motion, and equilibrium conceptually by identifying forces, representing force situations with diagrams and vectors, explaining Newton’s First Law, distinguishing balanced from unbalanced forces, and using evidence from real-world situations to determine whether an object’s motion should remain unchanged or change.

Section 1.1: Thinking Like a Physicist

Textbook Sections: 1.1, 1.2

Learning Target: Students will explain how physics uses observation, measurement, evidence, models, and testable claims to understand the natural world.

  • I can distinguish between an observation, inference, hypothesis, law, and theory.
  • I can explain why a claim must be testable to be scientific.
  • I can use evidence from a short scenario to decide whether a physics explanation is supported.
  • I can explain why formulas in physics are guides for thinking, not just tools for calculation.

Section 1.2: Forces and Newton’s First Law

Textbook Sections: 2.3, 2.6

Learning Target: Students will explain Newton’s First Law and use the idea of inertia to predict whether an object’s motion will stay the same or change.

  • I can state Newton’s First Law in everyday language.
  • I can explain that inertia is a property of matter, not a force.
  • I can distinguish between “no forces acting” and “no net force acting.”
  • I can identify support force, weight, and other common forces in simple situations.

Section 1.3: Net Force and Vectors

Textbook Sections: 2.4

Learning Target: Students will represent forces as vectors and determine the direction and relative size of the net force in one-dimensional and simple two-dimensional situations.

  • I can explain why force is a vector quantity.
  • I can combine forces acting in the same direction or opposite directions.
  • I can identify the resultant force from a simple vector diagram.
  • I can use arrow length and direction to compare force strength and direction.

Section 1.4: Equilibrium and Balanced Motion

Textbook Sections: 2.5, 2.7

Learning Target: Students will determine whether an object is in mechanical equilibrium and explain how balanced forces relate to rest or constant straight-line motion.

  • I can recognize static equilibrium and dynamic equilibrium.
  • I can explain why an object at rest can still have forces acting on it.
  • I can explain why an object moving at constant speed in a straight line can be in equilibrium.
  • I can use the equilibrium rule to reason about upward/downward and left/right forces.

Section 1.5: Modeling Forces in the Real World

Textbook Sections: 2.3–2.7 synthesis

Learning Target: Students will create and critique force models for real-world situations involving inertia, support force, net force, and equilibrium.

  • I can draw a force diagram for an object in a real-world situation.
  • I can decide whether the forces are balanced or unbalanced.
  • I can explain how the net force affects the object’s motion.
  • I can critique an incorrect force explanation and revise it using physics vocabulary.

Unit 1 Formula / Symbol Limit

This unit remains mostly conceptual. The following formulas and symbolic relationships are enough for the entire unit.

  1. Net force: $\Sigma F = F_{net}$
  2. Equilibrium rule: $\Sigma F = 0$
  3. Weight near Earth: $W = mg$
  4. Simple support-force balance on a level surface: $F_N = W$ when the object is at rest and no other vertical forces act.
  5. Same-direction forces: $F_{net}=F_1+F_2$
  6. Opposite-direction forces: $F_{net}=F_{larger}-F_{smaller}$, directed toward the larger force.

No more than these six formulas/symbolic relationships should be required for Unit 1 assessments.

Progress Tracking

A — Highly Proficient: I show strong, flexible understanding, use clear reasoning, connect force models to motion, and defend claims with evidence.

B — Proficient: I understand the essential ideas, complete familiar and moderately complex tasks accurately, and explain forces, net force, equilibrium, and Newton’s First Law.

C — Developing Proficiency: I understand the main ideas but still need support with diagrams, support force, dynamic equilibrium, or explaining force versus net force.

Not Yet — Insufficient Evidence: I do not yet show enough understanding and need more instruction, practice, or reassessment.

DOK 1 — Rungs 1–2

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, quick check, vocabulary check, card sort, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recognize basic force and motion vocabulary, identify simple motion states, and match conceptual terms to definitions or examples.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Vocabulary Match: Force, Inertia, and Equilibrium Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.2 — Students match terms to definitions, definitions to terms, and short concept examples to the correct term.
  2. Motion State Quick Sort Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.2 — Students sort examples into “at rest,” “constant straight-line motion,” or “changing motion.”
  3. Balanced or Unbalanced Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.4 — Students identify whether simple force pairs are balanced or unbalanced.
  4. Force Vocabulary in Context Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students read short everyday scenarios and choose the best term: inertia, net force, support force, weight, vector, or equilibrium.

What this tells the teacher:

Students who struggle here need more direct support with vocabulary, motion language, and the distinction between force, net force, and motion.

DOK 2 — Rungs 3–4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, whiteboard response, diagram task, or short calculation

Purpose: Check whether students can apply basic concepts to force diagrams, simple net force situations, and equilibrium reasoning.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Net Force Direction Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.3 — Students determine the size and direction of the net force from simple one-dimensional force diagrams.
  2. Force Diagram Labeling Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.3 — Students draw and label force vectors for a book on a table, a hanging sign, or a person standing on a scale.
  3. Support Force and Weight Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.4 — Students decide how the support force compares to weight in common equilibrium situations.
  4. Constant Motion Reasoning Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.4 — Students explain why an object moving at constant speed in a straight line can have zero net force even though forces act on it.

What this tells the teacher:

Students at this level can usually identify forces and apply the equilibrium rule in familiar situations. Errors reveal whether students are confusing individual forces with net force.

DOK 3 — Rungs 5–6

Format: Constructed response, misconception analysis, CER, ranking task, gallery walk, or group whiteboard defense

Purpose: Check whether students can reason strategically, explain cause and effect, connect diagrams to motion, and correct common misconceptions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Inertia Misconception Response Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.2 — Students respond to a claim such as, “Inertia is the force that keeps things moving,” and revise it.
  2. Multiple Representation Force Analysis Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students analyze a scenario, a force diagram, and a motion description, then explain whether they all agree.
  3. Bathroom Scale Explanation Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.4 — Students explain why a scale reading is not the same thing as net force and how support force relates to weight.
  4. Error Analysis: Balanced Forces Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students critique a student explanation that says, “If an object is moving, the forward force must be greater than the backward force.”

What this tells the teacher:

This level shows whether students understand the physics behind the vocabulary and calculations. These tasks are useful for deciding who is ready for modeling and who needs reteaching on net force, equilibrium, or Newton’s First Law.

DOK 4 — Rungs 7–8

Format: Performance task, extended response, modeling task, investigation reflection, or strategy defense

Purpose: Check whether students can synthesize force diagrams, evidence, motion descriptions, and conceptual explanations in unfamiliar real-world situations.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a Force Model for a Real Situation Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students choose a real object, describe its motion, draw a force model, and defend whether the forces are balanced or unbalanced.
  2. Competing Explanations Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students compare two explanations of the same scenario and defend which one better fits Newton’s First Law.
  3. Investigation-Based Claim-Evidence-Reasoning Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students use data or observations from a low-friction cart, tablecloth, hanging mass, or spring-scale investigation to explain inertia or equilibrium.
  4. Critique and Redesign a Force Diagram Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students evaluate a flawed force diagram, identify missing or incorrect forces, revise the diagram, and explain why the new model is better.

What this tells the teacher:

DOK 4 tasks show whether students can transfer conceptual understanding to unfamiliar situations and defend their reasoning using models and evidence.

Summative Assessment Plan

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute time limit.

The Unit 1 summative will be created as one consolidated HTML file with six versions of the assessment, an answer key, and a standardized two-page answer sheet. Test questions will be printed without workspaces so students use the answer sheet for responses and formula work.

Summative Assessment

Assessment File Structure

Single summative file: ../summative/unit1_summative_assessment/unit1_summative_assessment.html

The summative file will include six versions of the assessment. Each version should assess the same Unit 1 standards, DOK levels, vocabulary expectations, misconception checks, and formula skills, but with varied numbers, contexts, diagrams, wording, and answer order.

Recommended Student Assessment Structure

Questions 1–16: Multiple Choice — Students answer on the standardized bubble sheet.

  • 5 Questions DOK 1 — Vocabulary, motion-state recognition, term-to-definition, definition-to-term, concept-to-term, and term-to-concept.
  • 6 Questions DOK 2 — Interpreting force diagrams, identifying balanced/unbalanced forces, recognizing support force and weight, determining simple net force from diagrams, and applying the equilibrium rule.
  • 4 Questions DOK 3 — Analyzing misconceptions, connecting force diagrams to motion, interpreting support force and scale readings, comparing force situations, and choosing the best explanation.
  • 1 Question DOK 4 — Evaluating a model, critique, investigation-based reasoning, or choosing the best evidence-supported explanation.

Questions 17–20: Free Response / Formula and Reasoning Questions — These are the last four questions of each assessment version. Students complete these on the back side of the standardized answer sheet in four numbered quadrants.

  • Question 17: Formula / net-force reasoning using $\Sigma F$, same-direction forces, opposite-direction forces, or $W=mg$.
  • Question 18: Formula / support-force or equilibrium reasoning.
  • Question 19: Force diagram, model, or explanation connected to equilibrium or Newton’s First Law.
  • Question 20: Context-rich application, critique, or short CER-style reasoning task.

Answer Key and Answer Sheet Pages

After the six assessment versions, the summative file will include an answer key.

The final two pages of the file will be a standardized answer sheet:

  • Answer Sheet Page 1: Assessment Name, Student Name, bubble area for selecting version number, and bubbles for multiple-choice questions 1–16.
  • Answer Sheet Page 2: Prints on the back of Page 1 and is divided into four quadrants labeled 17, 18, 19, and 20 for the free-response formula and reasoning questions.

Question-Type Balance

  • Standard wording: about 5 questions
  • Inverted wording: about 5 questions
  • Context-rich wording: about 6 questions
  • Vocabulary / diagram / model-based wording: about 4 questions

Assessment Bank Note

The assessment bank should remain open-response, short-answer, diagram-based, and explanation-based. Bank items should not be written as multiple-choice questions. Selected bank items can later be adapted into multiple-choice summative questions with misconception-based distractors for questions 1–16.

Scoring Recommendation

Suggested weighting: Multiple Choice: 16 points; Free Response: 16 points total, 4 points per quadrant; Total: 32 points.

  • 4: Accurate answer, clear reasoning, correct vocabulary or diagram, and complete explanation.
  • 3: Mostly correct with minor error or missing detail.
  • 2: Partial understanding shown, but reasoning, diagram, or calculation is incomplete.
  • 1: Minimal correct physics shown.
  • 0: Blank, unrelated, or no meaningful evidence of understanding.
Holistic Rubric

A — Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 1 concepts. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student can explain inertia, Newton’s First Law, net force, support force, vectors, and equilibrium in familiar and unfamiliar situations. The student can draw and critique force diagrams, connect force models to motion, and defend claims using evidence.

B — Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 1 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. The student can identify forces, determine simple net force, explain balanced and unbalanced forces, and apply Newton’s First Law. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C — Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 1 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with force diagrams, support force, dynamic equilibrium, or explaining the difference between force and net force. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet — Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 1 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, confusion between force and motion, incorrect use of inertia, difficulty identifying net force, or weak connections between diagrams and motion. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
