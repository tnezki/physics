AP Calculus AB — Unit 1 Assessment Plan

Unit 1: Limits and Continuity

Essential Standard

Students will demonstrate AP Calculus AB proficiency for Unit 1 by interpreting concepts across graphs, tables, equations, and verbal contexts; selecting appropriate procedures; using precise notation; and writing clear AP-style justifications connected to the College Board topic sequence.

Section 1.1: What Is a Limit?

AP Topics 1.1, 1.2:

Learning Target: Students will interpret limits as values approached by a function and connect average versus instantaneous change, notation, graphs, tables, and verbal descriptions.

Unit 1 Formula / Symbol Limit

The following relationships and notation should be enough for the Unit 1 assessment plan. Students should be expected to explain what the notation means, not only use it procedurally.

  1. Limit notation: $\lim_{x\to a} f(x)=L$
  2. Continuity at a point: $\lim_{x\to a} f(x)=f(a)$
  3. Average rate of change: $\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$
  4. Vertical asymptote: $\lim_{x\to a} f(x)=\pm\infty$
  5. End behavior: $\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x)$
  6. IVT hypothesis: continuous on $[a,b]$

Progress Tracking

A — Highly Proficient: I show strong, flexible understanding, connect representations, select efficient strategies, and defend AP-style conclusions clearly.

B — Proficient: I understand the essential ideas, complete familiar and moderately complex tasks accurately, and explain my reasoning with correct notation.

C — Developing Proficiency: I understand the main ideas but still need support with representation connections, procedure selection, notation, or justification writing.

Not Yet — Insufficient Evidence: I do not yet show enough understanding and need more instruction, practice, or reassessment.

DOK 1 — Rungs 1–2

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, quick check, notation check, card sort, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recognize vocabulary, notation, representations, and basic meaning.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Vocabulary and Notation Quick Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.1 — Students match core terms, symbols, and meanings for what is a limit?.
  2. Representation Recognition Sort Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.2 — Students identify whether a graph, table, equation, or statement represents the targeted idea.
  3. Basic Skill Snapshot Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.3 — Students complete short routine items that check the unit's essential notation and procedures.
  4. Context Term Match Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students read brief AP-style statements and choose the correct vocabulary, theorem, or procedure.

What this tells the teacher:

DOK 1 evidence helps identify whether students are ready to move forward, need targeted reteaching, or are ready for more AP-style synthesis.

DOK 2 — Rungs 3–4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, AP Classroom-style topic question set, short calculation, or representation task

Purpose: Check whether students can apply familiar procedures and interpret standard representations.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Procedure Selection Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.3 — Students decide which rule, theorem, or representation is appropriate and complete a familiar application.
  2. Representation-to-Result Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.2 — Students use a graph, table, or formula to determine a requested value or conclusion.
  3. Mixed Routine Practice Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.4 — Students complete several short applications that require accuracy with unit procedures.
  4. Justification Starter Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students write a concise reason that supports a familiar conclusion using correct calculus language.

What this tells the teacher:

DOK 2 evidence helps identify whether students are ready to move forward, need targeted reteaching, or are ready for more AP-style synthesis.

DOK 3 — Rungs 5–6

Format: Constructed response, error analysis, graph/table explanation, mini-FRQ, or group whiteboard defense

Purpose: Check whether students can reason strategically, explain, connect representations, and correct errors.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Error Analysis Response Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.3 — Students identify and correct a common misconception or procedural error from the unit.
  2. Multiple Representation Analysis Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students connect graph, table, equation, and verbal information to defend a conclusion.
  3. AP-Style Explanation Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.4 — Students write a short AP-style justification with notation, units, and interpretation as appropriate.
  4. Strategy Comparison Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students compare two solution approaches and explain which is more efficient or better justified.

What this tells the teacher:

DOK 3 evidence helps identify whether students are ready to move forward, need targeted reteaching, or are ready for more AP-style synthesis.

DOK 4 — Rungs 7–8

Format: Performance task, extended response, modeling task, investigation reflection, or strategy defense

Purpose: Check whether students can synthesize unit ideas in unfamiliar AP-style contexts and defend conclusions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Model and Defend Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students build a model or solution plan for an unfamiliar problem and defend each step.
  2. Competing Solutions Critique Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students compare two student solutions, identify strengths and flaws, and revise the weaker work.
  3. Investigation-Based CER Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students use evidence from a graph, table, experiment, or scenario to make and defend a calculus claim.
  4. Transfer Performance Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 1, Section 1.5 — Students apply the unit ideas to a new AP-style context requiring synthesis and explanation.

Summative Assessment Plan

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute time limit.

The Unit 1 summative will be created as one consolidated HTML file with six versions of the assessment, an answer key, and a standardized two-page answer sheet. Test questions will be printed without large embedded workspaces so students use the answer sheet for responses and supporting work.

Summative Assessment

Assessment File Structure

Single summative file: ../summative/unit1_summative_assessment/unit1_summative_assessment.html

The summative file will include six versions of the assessment. Each version should assess the same Unit 1 standards, DOK levels, vocabulary expectations, representation skills, and AP-style justification habits, but with varied numbers, contexts, graphs, wording, and answer order.

Recommended Student Assessment Structure

Questions 1–16: Multiple Choice — Students answer on the standardized bubble sheet.

  • 4 Questions DOK 1 — Vocabulary, notation, representation recognition, and basic conceptual meaning.
  • 6 Questions DOK 2 — Routine procedures, graph/table interpretation, and familiar applications.
  • 5 Questions DOK 3 — Error analysis, multi-representation reasoning, interpretation, and AP-style explanation.
  • 1 Question DOK 4 — Transfer, model evaluation, or synthesis in an unfamiliar context.

Questions 17–20: Free Response / Reasoning Questions — These are the last four questions of each assessment version.

  • Question 17: Core procedure or notation task from Sections 1.1–1.2.
  • Question 18: Graph/table/formula interpretation task from Sections 1.2–1.4.
  • Question 19: AP-style explanation or justification task.
  • Question 20: Context-rich synthesis, critique, or short FRQ-style reasoning task.

Answer Key and Answer Sheet Pages

After the six assessment versions, the summative file will include an answer key. The final two pages should include a standardized answer sheet with version selection, multiple-choice bubbles, and four free-response spaces.

Assessment Bank

This bank is organized by DOK so future formative versions and summative versions can be assembled quickly. Bank questions should remain open-response, short-answer, graph/table-based, or explanation-based. Selected bank items can later be adapted into multiple-choice summative questions with misconception-based distractors.
